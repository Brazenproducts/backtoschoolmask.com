Complete Buying Guide — What to Know Before You Buy

N95 vs. KN95 vs. Surgical Mask — What the Labels Mean

NIOSH-approved N95 (with a TC approval number on the label) is the U.S. federal standard — tested and verified by the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health. The N95 label alone, without a TC number, is not verified. KN95 follows a Chinese standard with similar specifications but variable enforcement of testing requirements — buy from established manufacturers with documented test results. Surgical (3-ply) masks are fluid barriers, not respirators, and provide significantly lower filtration.

How to Verify a Genuine NIOSH N95

Every genuine NIOSH-approved N95 has a TC (Technical Certification) approval number printed on the mask and its packaging. You can verify this number directly on the NIOSH NPPTL database at cdc.gov/niosh. If the mask doesn't have a TC number or the number doesn't appear in the NIOSH database, it's not a genuine NIOSH-approved product regardless of what the label says.

Fit Testing and Seal for School-Age Children

Adult N95 masks don't seal correctly on children's faces — the filtration is useless without a proper face seal. For younger children (under 10), look specifically for child-size or 'small' rated masks. For teenagers, adult small or standard sizes typically fit. A proper seal means no gaps around the nose bridge, cheeks, and chin when breathing through the mask normally.

Comfort for Extended Classroom Wear

The difference between a mask a student will actually keep on versus one they'll remove every chance they get comes down to breathability and comfort. Cup-style N95s (like the YQ YICHITA) provide air space in front of the mouth, making breathing easier over long periods. Flat-fold designs sit closer to the face and are more compact but can feel warmer. Test a few styles before buying in bulk.

Mask Hygiene Practices for School Settings

Disposable masks are designed for one-use — they should be discarded after each school day or when visibly soiled or damp. Never store a used mask in a pocket or backpack where it contacts other surfaces. Send students with a clean mask each day and a small zip-lock bag for temporary removal during lunch. Reusable cloth masks require washing after every use.

Buying in Volume for a Full Semester

Calculate masks per week: 5 school days × 1 mask per day = 20 masks per month. For a 4-month semester, budget for 80 masks minimum. Buying 50–100 packs at the start of semester costs less per mask than buying smaller quantities throughout and ensures you have supply when demand spikes during cold season. Store sealed boxes in a cool, dry location.

Frequently Asked Questions

Back to School Masks & Respirators — Your Questions Answered

Are N95 masks necessary for school, or are surgical masks sufficient?

It depends on the health context. For most healthy students in typical classroom environments during normal flu season, a quality surgical mask provides meaningful protection against droplet transmission. For immunocompromised individuals, those in higher-density environments, or during active outbreaks of airborne pathogens, NIOSH-approved N95 provides substantially better protection.

How can I tell if an N95 mask is fake?

Check for a NIOSH TC approval number printed directly on the mask (not just the box). Verify that number at cdc.gov/niosh/npptl/topics/respirators/disp_part/n95list1.html. Genuine NIOSH N95s are never sold as 'N95 style' or 'N95 standard.' They're either NIOSH-approved with a verified TC number or they aren't N95 products.

What masks are safe for children under 12?

Look specifically for masks labeled 'small' or 'child size' with documented filtration testing. Adult masks won't seal on children's faces, defeating their purpose. YW well-fitted KN95 or surgical masks from reputable manufacturers that offer pediatric sizing are appropriate. Consult your child's doctor for specific medical needs.

How long can a student wear an N95 in class?

N95 respirators can be worn for a full school day in most cases. Extended wear may cause mild discomfort — breathing resistance increases slightly with mask saturation. Replace the mask if it becomes visibly soiled, damp from breathing, or if the seal starts to feel compromised. Single-use N95s should be discarded at end of day.

Can masks be reused across multiple school days?

Single-use disposable masks (N95, KN95, surgical) are designed for one-day use and should be discarded. Reuse increases contamination risk and reduces filtration efficiency as the mask absorbs moisture and debris. Multi-day reuse of N95s is sometimes accepted in supply-constrained medical settings but is not the recommended practice.

What's the best mask for a student with glasses?

Glasses-wearers need masks with effective nose wire adjustability to prevent fogging. N95 cup-style masks with a formable nose bridge are the best option — they seal closely to the nose without the gaps that cause glasses to fog. The flat-fold 3M 8210 also performs well for glasses-wearers who adjust the nose wire carefully.

Are cloth masks effective enough for school?

Reusable cloth masks offer variable protection depending on material, layering, and fit. Well-fitted, multi-layer cloth masks with a nose wire and snug seal provide meaningful reduction in exposure — but significantly less than a properly worn N95. They're a reasonable everyday option for healthy students in low-risk environments, but not adequate protection for higher-risk situations.

Should teachers wear N95 masks in classrooms?

Teachers who spend extended periods in high-density indoor environments are at elevated exposure risk. NIOSH-approved N95 masks provide meaningfully better protection than surgical masks and are appropriate for teachers with health concerns or those in higher-risk teaching environments. The CDC and OSHA both recognize N95s as a higher level of respiratory protection.

How should masks be stored between uses?

For single-use masks, dispose of them at end of day. For brief temporary removal (lunch, outdoor break), store the mask in a clean paper bag — not a plastic bag, which traps moisture and promotes bacterial growth. Never fold or compress a cup-style N95 — it deforms the seal. Keep clean masks in the original packaging until needed.

What's the most cost-effective approach for a family buying masks for the full school year?

Calculate your total need (5 days/week × number of students × school weeks), then buy a 50–100 count box at start of semester. The per-mask cost drops significantly in larger quantities. For two kids in school for 36 weeks: 360 masks minimum — a 4-box purchase of 100-count is more economical than buying 20-packs throughout the year.
